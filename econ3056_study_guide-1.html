What Are Relevant Cash Flows?
A relevant cash flow is one that will change as a direct result of the decision about a project — the difference in the firm's cash flows with the project versus without it.

Two golden rules determine relevance:

  • Future-oriented: A cash flow must occur in the future to be relevant. Anything already spent is irrelevant.
  • Incremental: Only changes caused directly by the project count. Costs that stay the same regardless are not relevant.
⚠️ Sunk Costs are NEVER relevant. Money already spent cannot be recovered and must be ignored in project decisions, no matter how large it was.
📐
The 3 Guiding Principles — Learn These Cold

① The Stand-Alone (Incremental) Principle

Evaluate the project purely on its own merits, in isolation from any other activities. Calculate incremental cash flows by comparing the firm's total future cash flows with and without the project. Only incremental costs count.
Example: A firm considers a new product. The salary of the CFO who will oversee it is not a relevant cost — he is paid regardless. But hiring a new project manager specifically for this product is relevant.

② The Indirect / Synergistic Effect

Include indirect effects a project has on other parts of the firm. These can be negative (cannibalization) or positive (cross-selling). The rationale rests on the opportunity cost principle.
Classic Example (from class): A car maker introduces model "Mako," a substitute for existing model "Raptor." Expected Raptor sales drop by $25M over the project's life. This must be included as a cash outflow — it's a real cost of the Mako project.

③ The Opportunity Cost Principle

Resources have value measured by their best forgone alternative use. Even when no cash changes hands, an opportunity cost exists and must be included.
Classic Example (from class): A firm uses its own factory space (market rental value: $29,000/yr) for a new production facility. The space isn't rented now, but could be. The $29,000/yr must be included as a cash outflow — the firm forgoes rental income by using the space.
Trick question trap: If a firm owns land and will still own it after the project, does that land have an opportunity cost? YES — if it could be sold for $500,000, that is the opportunity cost and should be included as an outlay.

The Capital Budgeting Process — Key Steps
1
Strategic Planning
Translates corporate goals into specific policies and priorities. Investment proposals must align with the firm's vision and long-term strategy. There is two-way feedback: a great project can change strategy.
2
Investment Opportunity Identification
Projects emerge from R&D, employees, advisors, or external sources. They must align with corporate goals — though exceptional opportunities can prompt a strategy revision.
3
Preliminary Screening (Feasibility)
Isolates marginal and unsound projects. Covers 8 feasibility types: Market, Technical, Resource, Cultural, Operational, Legal, Schedule, and Economic feasibility.
4
Financial Appraisal
Quantitative analysis — forecasting cash flows, NPV analysis, sensitivity analysis, simulation. Provides estimated addition to firm value. For public projects, also requires Social CBA.
5
The Accept / Reject Decision
Management weighs NPV results plus qualitative factors (environmental, social, political impact), combined with experience and judgment.
6
Implementation & Monitoring
Constant monitoring for bottlenecks and deviations from projected cash flows. Post-mortem of completed projects feeds back into future appraisals and strategy.

The Three Financial Decisions of the Firm

Financial Management is concerned with three core decisions, all oriented toward the firm's goal of wealth maximisation.

① Investment Decision

How should the firm allocate capital into long-term assets? This is the Capital Budgeting decision — which projects to accept or reject. It directly determines the productive capacity and future cash flows of the firm.

② Financing Decision

How should the firm raise the funds needed for its investments? This is the debt/equity mix decision — choosing the optimal capital structure (what proportion of debt vs. equity to use).

③ Dividend Decision

What proportion of earnings should be returned to shareholders as dividends vs. retained and reinvested in the firm? The dividend payout ratio affects shareholder wealth and future investment capacity.

How Capital Budgeting Fits In

Capital Budgeting is the execution of the Investment Decision. It is the process by which the firm identifies, evaluates, and selects long-term investments that are expected to yield benefits beyond one year — directly serving the firm's goal of maximising shareholder wealth.

Investment Project Classifications
1
Independent Projects
Acceptance or rejection of one project does not affect the decision on any other project. Each stands alone on its own merits.
Example: A hospital decides to install a new MRI machine AND renovate its car park. These decisions are unrelated — one does not depend on the other.
2
Mutually Exclusive Projects
Two or more projects that cannot be pursued simultaneously. Accepting one automatically eliminates the others. Common when resources are limited.
Example: A company owns a plot of land and must choose between building a warehouse OR a car park. Doing one means the other is impossible. Projects A and B in the class NPV example are mutually exclusive.
3
Contingent Projects
Acceptance or rejection depends on the decision made about one or more other projects. These may be complementary or substitutes.
Example: A firm decides to purchase new manufacturing machinery (Project A). The decision to also buy a software system to run that specific machinery (Project B) is contingent on accepting Project A — Project B is useless without it.
4
Substitute Projects
The degree of success of one project is increased by rejecting another. Accepting both reduces each other's value.
Example: A company opening a new branch in the same neighbourhood as an existing branch — the new branch cannibalises existing customers, making both less successful than either would be alone.

Project Cash Flow Categories

Capital Cash Flows

  • Initial Investment (I₀): The largest single outflow at t=0. Includes asset purchase/creation, installation costs, and initial working capital.
  • Additional Investment: Upgrades or working capital increases during the project life.
  • Terminal Cash Flows: At project end — salvage/liquidation value, recovery of working capital, asset disposal costs, environmental remediation, redundancy payments.

Operating Cash Flows

  • Generated continuously over the project's economic life.
  • Cash Inflows: Sales receipts, rental income, etc.
  • Cash Outflows: Advertising, marketing, utility costs, manpower costs, raw material purchases, etc.
  • Net Operating Cash Flow = Inflows − Outflows for each period.
Key exam distinction: Capital flows are typically one-off, large, and tied to the beginning or end of the project. Operating flows are recurring throughout the project's life.

Asset Types & Investment Classifications

Tangible vs. Intangible Assets

Tangible Assets

Physical, touchable assets. Examples: property, plant, machinery, equipment, vehicles, buildings. Their value is visible and relatively straightforward to assess.

Intangible Assets

Non-physical assets. Examples: patents, trademarks, new technology, brand names, software. Also includes investments in R&D, design, development and testing — through which new products and technology are created.

Asset Expansion vs. Asset Replacement

Asset Expansion

Investment in additional assets to expand an existing product/service line, enter a new line of business, increase sales, or reduce costs. The firm is growing its productive capacity.

Example: A bakery adding a second oven to increase production volume.

Asset Replacement

Retirement of an existing asset and replacement with a newer, more efficient one. Includes asset retirement, abandonment, and disposal of the old asset.

Example: A delivery company replacing aging trucks with newer, fuel-efficient models.

Mandatory vs. Discretionary Investments

Mandatory Investments

Required to satisfy regulatory, health, or safety requirements. The firm has no real choice — these are essential to remain in business or legally compliant.

Example: Installing fire suppression systems to meet building codes.

Discretionary Investments

Generated by growth opportunities, competition, or cost reduction. These are the firm's strategic choices and normally represent its long-term plan.

Example: Launching a new product line, entering a foreign market, automating a production process.

Net Present Value (NPV) Method
The NPV is the net monetary gain or loss from a project, computed by discounting all present and future cash inflows and outflows back to time 0 (the start of the project).
NPV Formula
NPV = -I₀ + Σ [NCFₜ × (1 + i)⁻ᵗ] + [LV × (1 + i)⁻ᵀ]

Where:
  I₀ = Initial Investment (time 0)
  NCFₜ = Net Cash Flow in period t
  i = Discount rate (interest rate)
  t = Time period
  T = End of project life
  LV = Liquidation Value at end of life
  (1+i)⁻ᵗ = Discounting factor for period t
Absolute Profitability
Project is acceptable if NPV > 0. A positive NPV means the project generates more than the cost of capital — it adds value.
Relative Profitability
When choosing between projects, select the one with the higher NPV.

Strengths & Weaknesses of NPV (common essay component)

✅ Strengths

  • Widely known and used
  • Accounts for time value of money
  • Considers all cash flows over project life
  • Relatively low computational effort
  • Clear decision rule (NPV > 0 = accept)

❌ Weaknesses / Limiting Assumptions

  • All data assumed to be certain — unrealistic
  • Requires a perfect capital market (borrow/invest at same rate, unlimited)
  • Economic life must be pre-determined
  • Assumes reinvestment of surpluses at the discount rate
  • Single target measure may be inadequate
  • Cash flows may not be allocable to clean periods

The Annuity Method
An annuity is a series of equal cash flows over equal time periods. The Annuity Method converts the NPV into an equivalent annual amount — making comparison between projects of different lives fair and easy.
Annuity Formula (Capital Recovery Factor)
ANN = NPV × [ (1+i)ᵀ × i ] / [ (1+i)ᵀ − 1 ]

Where ANN = Annual equivalent value of the project
The term in brackets is the Capital Recovery Factor (CRF)
🎯
When to use Annuity over NPV: Use the Annuity method when comparing projects with different economic lives, OR when a project repeats identically and indefinitely (since no NPV can be determined for infinite time horizons).
Worked Example
Annuity Calculation — Projects A & B (5yr vs 4yr lives)

Because Project A runs 5 years and Project B runs 4 years, we cannot fairly compare their NPVs directly. The Annuity (annual equivalent value) creates a level playing field.

1
Apply the Capital Recovery Factor to each project's NPV
ANNA = €26,771.59 × [ (1.08)⁵ × 0.08 ] / [ (1.08)⁵ − 1 ]
         = €26,771.59 × [ 1.4693 × 0.08 ] / [ 1.4693 − 1 ]
         = €26,771.59 × 0.11746 / 0.46933
         = €26,771.59 × 0.25046
         = €6,705.15 per year
ANNB = €25,469.32 × [ (1.08)⁴ × 0.08 ] / [ (1.08)⁴ − 1 ]
         = €25,469.32 × 0.30192
         = €7,689.72 per year
2
Interpret — which project is better?
ANNB (€7,690) > ANNA (€6,705) → Project B is relatively more profitable on an annualized basis. Even though Project A had a higher raw NPV, Project B generates more value per year, which matters when you assume the projects can be repeated.

This reversal is the entire reason the annuity method exists — NPV alone can mislead when project lives differ.

3

NPV (infinite chain) = ANN / i

Annuity > 0 → accept the project
Relative Profitability
Higher annuity = better project (especially across different lives)

Dynamic Payback Period (DPP)
The DPP is the number of periods needed for discounted net cash inflows to fully recover the initial investment. It is the discounted version of the simple payback period — it uses present values, not raw cash flows.
DPP Method — How it works
1. Calculate the cumulative NPV at the end of each period.
2. The period in which cumulative NPV = 0 is the DPP.
3. If cumulative NPV is still negative → investment not yet recovered.
4. If cumulative NPV turns zero or positive → DPP achieved.
Concept Example
DPP — Tracking Cumulative Discounted Cash Flow

Using Project A data (I₀ = €100,000, i = 8%):

Period NCF (€) PV of NCF (€) Cumulative NPV (€) Status
0−100,000−100,000−100,000❌ Not recovered
1+28,000+25,926−74,074❌
2+30,000+25,720−48,354❌
3+35,000+27,784−20,570❌
4+32,000+23,521+2,951✅ DPP achieved!
5+35,000+23,821+26,772Final NPV
The DPP is reached during year 4 — that is when the cumulative discounted cash flows first turn positive, recovering the full initial investment.

✅ Advantage over Simple Payback

Uses discounted (present value) cash flows, so it correctly accounts for the time value of money — unlike the static payback period.

❌ Key Limitation

Ignores all cash flows after the DPP is reached. A project with massive late-period gains could be rejected. Therefore, it is a risk measure, not a standalone profitability criterion.

📌
Special case: DPP gives the same result as NPV for absolute profitability only when the designated payback limit equals the economic life of the project.
📈
Internal Rate of Return (IRR)
The IRR is the discount rate that makes the NPV of a project equal to zero. It represents the rate of return earned on capital employed by the investment.
Definition
Solve for r* such that:
NPV = −I₀ + Σ [NCFₜ × (1 + r*)⁻ᵗ] + [LV × (1 + r*)⁻ᵀ] = 0
Absolute Profitability
Accept if IRR > uniform discount rate (i)
Relative Profitability
Choose project with higher IRR
⚠️ Critical Weakness — The Reinvestment Assumption: The IRR method assumes all surplus cash flows can be reinvested at exactly the IRR rate. This is generally unrealistic. When projects differ in scale or economic life, the IRR can give misleading relative profitability rankings — a smaller project with lower capital tie-up often "wins" under IRR, even if the NPV comparison would favor the larger project.

Decision Rules — Unknown Probabilities

When you cannot estimate the probability of each scenario, use these rules. They work on a Decision Matrix where rows = alternatives (Aⱼ) and columns = scenarios (Sᵤ).

Example Decision Matrix (NPV in $000s)
AlternativeS₁S₂S₃S₄S₅Min (Maximin)Max (Maximax)
A₁10020030050−50−50300
A₂115180150115115115 ← MAX180
A₃0250400−100200−100400 ← MAX
A₄80906070505090

① Maximin Rule — The Pessimist's Choice

Find the minimum NPV in each row (worst case for each alternative), then select the alternative with the highest minimum. You are maximizing your worst-case outcome.
From the matrix: The row minima are −50, 115, −100, 50. The maximum of these is 115 → Select A₂.

Who uses this? A highly risk-averse investor who assumes the worst will always happen.
Weakness: Only looks at one scenario per alternative. Ignores the possibility of great outcomes.

② Maximax Rule — The Optimist's Choice

Find the maximum NPV in each row (best case), then select the alternative with the highest maximum. You are chasing the best possible outcome.
From the matrix: The row maxima are 300, 180, 400, 90. The maximum is 400 → Select A₃.

Weakness: Completely ignores risk. A₃'s worst case is −100, which could be catastrophic.

③ Hurwicz Rule — The Balanced Choice (Optimism-Pessimism Rule)

A weighted combination of Maximin and Maximax using an optimism coefficient α (between 0 and 1).

Hurwicz Score = α × (row maximum) + (1−α) × (row minimum)

α = 1 → pure Maximax. α = 0 → pure Maximin.
Example
Hurwicz with α = 0.4
AlternativeMaxMinScore = 0.4×Max + 0.6×Min
A₁300−500.4(300)+0.6(−50) = 120−30 = 90 ← Highest
A₂1801150.4(180)+0.6(115) = 72+69 = 141
A₃400−1000.4(400)+0.6(−100) = 160−60 = 100
A₄90500.4(90)+0.6(50) = 36+30 = 66
With α = 0.4, A₁ scores highest (141) → Select A₁. Note how the choice changes based on α — this is the method's flexibility and also its challenge (α is subjective).
📊
Decision Rules — Known Probabilities (Bayes Rule)
When you can estimate the probability of each scenario, use the expected value. For each alternative, multiply each NPV by its probability and sum the results.

E(Aⱼ) = Σ [ NPVⱼᵤ × pᵤ ]

Select the alternative with the highest expected value.
Example
Bayes Expected Value Calculation

Suppose three alternatives with two scenarios: S₁ (p=0.6) and S₂ (p=0.4)

AlternativeNPV under S₁ (p=0.6)NPV under S₂ (p=0.4)E(A) = Σ NPV×p
A₁$200$500.6(200)+0.4(50) = 120+20 = $140
A₂$150$1800.6(150)+0.4(180) = 90+72 = $162 ← Select
A₃$300−$1000.6(300)+0.4(−100) = 180−40 = $140
Select A₂ with E(A₂) = $162.

Assumption: Bayes rule assumes a risk-neutral attitude — that utility is proportional to monetary value. It ignores the spread/variance of outcomes.

Sensitivity & Breakeven Analysis ★ Explicitly Hinted
Sensitivity Analysis: Systematically change the value of one forecasted variable at a time and observe the resulting change in NPV. Variables that cause large NPV changes are called sensitive variables.

Breakeven Analysis: Find the specific value of a variable that gives the project an NPV of exactly zero — the breakeven point. Variables are often called breakeven values.

The Process

1
Identify Variables Likely to Impact NPV
Examples: unit selling price, sales volume, operating costs, project life, discount rate, initial investment. Choose variables based on degree of management control and confidence in forecasts.

✅ Strengths

  • Easy to understand and explain
  • Forces planning discipline
  • Highlights the riskiest variables
  • Relatively cheap to perform

❌ Weaknesses

  • Relatively unsophisticated
  • Only changes one variable at a time
  • Ignores interdependencies between variables
  • May not capture all information

Risk-Adjusted Discount Rate & WACC
Risk in project analysis = variation in future cash flows around an expected value. One way to handle this is to use a higher discount rate that includes a risk premium.
Risky Discount Rate
k = r + u + a

r = risk-free rate (time value of money)
u = average risk premium (firm's general business risk)
a = additional factor (positive, zero, or negative) for
    this project's risk relative to the firm's average risk

Calculating WACC

The Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) is the overall rate of return required to satisfy all suppliers of capital. It can be used as the risky discount rate k.
Worked Example
WACC Calculation (from class)
Capital SourceWeightRequired ReturnAfter-Tax RateWeighted Cost
Common Stock50%10%10%0.50 × 0.10 = 0.050
Preferred Stock10%8%8%0.10 × 0.08 = 0.008
Long-Term Debt40%7% (pre-tax)7%(1−0.30) = 4.9%0.40 × 0.049 = 0.0196
WACC =7.76%
Note the tax adjustment on debt: Interest on debt is tax-deductible, so its real cost to the firm is reduced. The after-tax cost = pre-tax rate × (1 − tax rate). Equity has no such adjustment.

Certainty Equivalent Method

Instead of adjusting the discount rate, adjust the cash flows themselves to their "certain" equivalent, then discount at the risk-free rate.

NPV = Σ [ b × CF_t / (1+r)ᵗ ] − C₀

Where b = certainty coefficient (set by management, between 0 and 1). A lower b means greater uncertainty — the cash flow is adjusted downward to reflect risk.

Financial Analysis vs. Economic Analysis
Though both aim to appraise investment profitability, they differ fundamentally in scope and perspective.
CriteriaFinancial AnalysisEconomic Analysis
FocusProfitability for project ownersNational economic interest
CostsOnly costs relevant to the projectIncludes externalities & economy-wide costs
BenefitsOnly benefits to ownersBenefits to the national economy
ValuationMarket priceWillingness to pay & opportunity costs
CoverageNarrowBroad
ViabilityFinancial viability onlyFinancial viability necessary but not sufficient
Key Exam Point: A project can be financially viable (profitable to the owner) but not economically viable (if the social costs outweigh social benefits). Economic analysis is broader — it asks "is this good for society?"
📋
What is Cost-Benefit Analysis?
CBA is a policy assessment method that quantifies the value of policy consequences in monetary terms to all members of society. It calculates Net Social Benefits (NSB) for each policy alternative:

NSB = Social Benefits (B) − Social Costs (C)

Decision Rule: Investments with positive net benefits are considered acceptable.

CBA begins with a financial statement and makes two key adjustments:

  • Certain costs and benefits are included or excluded (e.g., transfers between parties cancel out at the societal level)
  • The valuation of items changes from market prices to economic values (opportunity costs, willingness to pay)
🕐
Types of CBA — Know All Four
TypeTimingPrimary UseQuality of Info
Ex Ante Before the intervention Resource allocation decisions — should we invest here? Poorest (predictions only)
In Medias Res During the intervention Redirect resources if needed; mid-course corrections Moderate
Ex Post After the intervention Learning actual project value; informing similar future projects Best (actual outcomes known)
Comparative Ex ante vs. Ex post Evaluate accuracy of ex ante predictions; understand forecast errors Varies
💡
Key distinction to know: Ex Post is best for learning but "too late" for resource reallocation. Ex Ante is when you can actually decide — but it relies on uncertain predictions. In Medias Res is a compromise.
🪜
The 9 Steps of CBA — Know the Order
Step 1
Specify Alternatives
Define the full set of alternative projects, including a "do nothing" option as baseline.
Step 2
Decide on Standing
Determine whose costs and benefits count — local, national, or global perspective.
Step 3
Identify Impacts
Catalogue all impact categories (economic, social, environmental) and select measurement indicators.
Step 4
Predict Impacts
Forecast all impacts quantitatively over the entire project life.
Step 5
Monetize All Impacts
Convert all impacts to monetary values (including non-market goods like lives saved, time saved).
Step 6
Discount to PV
Discount all future benefits and costs to Present Values using the appropriate social discount rate.
Step 7
Compute Net PV
Calculate NPV (or Net Social Benefit) for each alternative: NSB = PV(Benefits) − PV(Costs).
Step 8
Sensitivity Analysis
Test how robust results are to changes in key assumptions — especially monetization values and the discount rate.
Step 9
Make a Recommendation
Present findings and recommend the option with the highest positive Net Social Benefit.
👥
Project Perspectives — Guardian, Spender & Society

Different stakeholders evaluate the same project through very different lenses. Understanding these perspectives — and their biases — is critical for economic analysis.

🛡️
The Guardian
  • Found in Central Budgetary Agencies
  • Views projects through a revenue vs. expenditure lens
  • Revenue inflows = "benefits"; expenditure outflows = "costs"
  • Tends to regard CBA as naïve and impractical
  • Ignores non-financial social costs/benefits (time saved, lives, pollution)
  • Views government-owned resources as free (no opportunity cost)
  • Treats subsidies from other governments as "benefits" — incorrect under CBA
  • Prefers a high discount rate (similar to market rates)
  • Ignores costs not borne by own level of government
💸
The Spender
  • Found in service or line departments
  • Views expenditures on constituents as "benefits"
  • Transfers received by constituents are called "benefits" — incorrect
  • Views worker wages as benefits (supports any project)
  • Government-owned resources have no cost in their view
  • Favors large, capital-intensive projects (harder to cancel)
  • Rejects market allocation principles
  • Prefers a low discount rate (raises NPV of their projects)
🌍
Society
  • The correct economic perspective — CBA represents this view
  • Two primary, equally-valued objectives:
  • Growth Objective: Increase total national income
  • Equity Objective: Improve the distribution of national income
  • Values all costs and benefits at opportunity cost / willingness to pay
  • Includes externalities (pollution, time saved, lives)
  • Uses the social discount rate (typically lower than market rates)
⚠️
Key Exam Contrast: The Guardian and Spender both misidentify costs and benefits in systematic ways.
• The Guardian ignores social costs — making projects seem cheaper than they are.
• The Spender inflates benefits — making projects seem more valuable than they are.
• Society's perspective (CBA) corrects both biases by using opportunity costs and including all externalities.
⚠️
Difficulties in Public Project CBA
  • Identifying all users who can benefit from the project — some benefits are diffuse and hard to trace
  • Identifying all benefits and dis-benefits — many are non-market (environmental, social)
  • Quantifying non-market benefits and dis-benefits in dollar terms — e.g., how do you value a human life or a year without pollution?
  • Selecting an appropriate social discount rate — Guardians want it high (fewer projects pass), Spenders want it low (more projects pass)
